US Bond Market — Overview
Agency Bond Market
Securities Overview i
Agency Securities

Aggregated daily trading volume for each Agency bond issuer in $B notional. Agency bonds are debt issued by US government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs) to fund their lending programs (mortgages, farm credit, etc.).

FNMA (Fannie Mae): funds conforming mortgages via MBS guarantee.

FHLB: Federal Home Loan Banks — advance funding to member depository institutions.

FHLMC (Freddie Mac): similar to FNMA, secondary mortgage market participant.

GNMA (Ginnie Mae): guarantees MBS backed by FHA/VA loans — explicit US government guarantee.

Trade Type Breakdown i
Trade Type Breakdown

FINRA TRACE classifies each bond transaction by counterparty type, revealing who is driving market liquidity.

Affiliate: trades between a broker-dealer and its affiliated entities. Buy/Sell/Net reveals intra-group positioning.

Customer: trades between a dealer and an end-investor (asset manager, insurer, pension). Buy = client bought from dealer; Sell = client sold to dealer.

Inter-dealer: trades between two registered broker-dealers — the wholesale layer of bond market liquidity.

Corporate 144A Bond Market
Securities Overview i
Corp 144A Securities

Rule 144A bonds are private placements sold only to Qualified Institutional Buyers (QIBs, ≥$100M AUM). Not SEC-registered but freely resold among QIBs — faster to issue than public bonds.

Investment Grade (IG): rated BBB-/Baa3 or above. Lower default risk, tight spreads over Treasuries.

High Yield (HY): rated below BBB- ("junk"). Higher coupon, wider spreads, elevated default risk.

Convertibles: bonds convertible into equity at a preset price. Hybrid risk — equity optionality + fixed income floor.

EU Bond Market — Overview
EU Government & Corporate Bonds
Securities Overview i
EU Bond Securities

EGB (European Government Bonds): sovereign debt from EU member states (Bunds, OATs, BTPs, BONOs…). The German Bund is the eurozone risk-free benchmark.

Corp IG: investment-grade EUR corporate bonds — lower risk, tight spreads vs EGBs.

Corp HY: sub-investment-grade European corporate bonds. Wider spreads, higher default risk.

Covered Bonds: bank bonds backed by mortgage/public sector pools. Very high credit quality (Pfandbriefe, Obligations Foncières). Distinct legal protection for investors.

EU Trade Types

EU bond markets use MiFID II post-trade reporting. Counterparty classification mirrors FINRA TRACE logic.

Affiliate: intra-group transactions (bank subsidiaries, dealer affiliates).

Customer: end-investor (pension, insurer, fund) buying or selling through a primary dealer or electronic venue (MTS, Bloomberg).

Inter-dealer: liquidity provision and hedging between market-making banks. Key driver of EU bond market depth.

China Bond Market — Overview
China Interbank & Exchange Bond Market
Securities Overview i
China Bond Securities

CGB (Chinese Gov Bonds): Ministry of Finance sovereign debt. The 10Y CGB is China's risk-free benchmark rate.

Policy Bank Bonds: issued by CDB, EXIM Bank, ADBC. Quasi-sovereign — fund national policy (infrastructure, agriculture). AAA-rated, minimal credit risk.

Local Gov Bonds (LGBs): provincial/municipal debt to fund infrastructure. Volume surged post-2015 swap reforms. Risk varies by province.

Corp Credit: onshore CNY corporate bonds. Dominated by state-owned enterprises (SOEs) with implied government support.

 ¥B notional
Trade Type Breakdown i
China Trade Types

China's market is bifurcated: the Interbank market (CFETS) handles ~90% of volume; the Exchange market (SSE/SZSE) is accessible to retail.

Affiliate: intra-group trades within financial conglomerates (bank subsidiaries, trust companies).

Customer: domestic funds, insurers, and foreign institutions via Bond Connect or CIBM Direct.

Inter-dealer: repo and outright transactions between commercial banks and securities firms on CFETS — the backbone of CNY bond liquidity.

Japan Bond Market — Overview
JGB & Corporate Bond Market
Securities Overview i
Japan Bond Securities

JGB (Japanese Gov Bonds): MoF sovereign debt. The BOJ holds ~50% of outstanding JGBs after years of YCC (Yield Curve Control). Liquidity has been structurally impaired.

T-Bills: short-term (3M, 6M) government securities. Heavily used in repo markets and yen liquidity management.

Corp Bonds: JPY-denominated corporate debt. Japanese IG corporates have very tight spreads and low historical default rates.

Samurai / Supra: JPY bonds issued by foreign governments or supranationals (World Bank, EIB) in Japan — attracted by deep investor base.

Yield Curve — Short vs Long Term
Government Yield Curve i
Yield Curve

The yield curve plots government bond yields from the shortest (3M) to longest maturity (30Y). Its shape encodes the market's expectation of growth, inflation, and central bank policy.

Normal (upward-sloping): long yields > short yields. Healthy growth / inflation expected. Positive term premium compensates for duration risk.

Inverted: short yields > long yields. The Fed is tight, and markets expect eventual rate cuts — a historically reliable recession signal.

Flat / Humped: uncertainty about the cycle. Often seen at inflection points.

2Y–10Y Spread History
Spread Evolution i
2Y–10Y Spread

The 2Y vs 10Y yield spread is Wall Street's most-watched inversion signal.

Negative (inverted): the short end yields more — market expects the central bank to cut rates because a slowdown is coming. Has preceded every US recession since 1955 (with ~12–18 month lead).

Positive (steepening): normal curve. Wide spreads signal strong growth expectations or rising inflation risk.

The red shaded zones mark inversion periods. A return to positive territory ("bear steepener" or "bull steepener") is an inflection point to watch.

Options & Futures — Market Structure
Futures Curve — Contango / Backwardation i
Futures Curve Structure

This heatmap shows the roll spread (far contract price minus near contract price) across tenors and expiry months.

Contango (green): far contracts > near contracts. The futures curve slopes upward. Carry is positive — rolling a long position forward costs the roll. Common in normal markets.

Backwardation (red): near contracts > far contracts. Demand for near-term delivery is high (e.g. short squeeze, repo stress, delivery squeeze). Rolling a long earns the roll — "positive roll yield". Often signals market stress.

Cell = futures price. Sub-number = roll spread. Color intensity = magnitude. Use for calendar spread strategies and optimal roll timing.

Vol Surface — Swaption Matrix i
Swaption Vol Surface

A swaption is an option to enter into an interest rate swap. This matrix maps ATM (at-the-money) normal implied volatility in basis points across two axes.

Rows = Expiry: time until the option expires (1M → 5Y). Short expiries are sensitive to central bank meeting risk.

Columns = Tenor: length of the underlying swap (1Y → 30Y). Long tenors embed long-run rate uncertainty.

Dark cells = low vol: market sees low rate uncertainty in that region.
Bright cells = high vol: elevated hedging demand. Use to identify where rate risk is priced most aggressively — and where vol is cheap or rich relative to history.

Open Interest & Volume Ratio i
Open Interest & Volume

Open Interest (OI): total outstanding futures contracts not yet settled. Changes in OI reveal new position creation vs. liquidation.

Rising price + rising OI: new longs entering — bullish conviction.
Falling price + rising OI: new shorts added — bearish positioning.
Falling OI: position unwinding / liquidation regardless of direction.

Volume: contracts traded intra-session. High volume confirms price moves; low volume signals less conviction.

OI/Volume Ratio: ratio >1 means position buildup exceeds turnover — strong directional commitment. Ratio <1 suggests roll activity or speculative churn.

Multi-Country Sovereign Curves

Superimposing government bond curves allows direct comparison of monetary policy stance, term premium, and sovereign credit across markets.

Countries with a steep front-end (US, UK) are in aggressive tightening cycles. Japan's near-zero short end reflects the BOJ's ultra-accommodative stance (YCC gradually being unwound).

Spread compression between two 10Y yields (e.g., BTP vs Bund) is the key barometer of eurozone stress — widening signals political or fiscal risk in the peripheral.

10Y Spread vs US Treasury
Sovereign Spread Matrix i
Sovereign Spread Matrix

The spread vs UST 10Y measures yield premium (or discount) over US Treasuries at each maturity point.

Negative spread: the country yields less than the US — typically Germany, Japan, Switzerland. Reflects a different rate cycle, currency safe-haven status, or lower inflation expectations.

Positive spread: yield premium over Treasuries. Reflects higher credit risk, inflation expectations, or a currency risk premium for USD investors on an unhedged basis.

The 1W Δ (bps) column shows the weekly change — spread widening = deterioration, spread tightening = improvement in relative terms.
